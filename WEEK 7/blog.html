June 16, 1999

TRUE CRIME IN THE 1900's

True crime stories have a firm hold on the popular imagination with an ever-increasing number of books, television shows, and podcasts devoted to the subject. This interest has played out in both fiction and nonfiction. It might seem a distinctly modern preoccupation stemming from advances in forensic science that make it possible to precisely analyze the elements of a crime scene and reconstruct what happened. However, this fascination goes back at least to the nineteenth century when New York newspapers of the 1890s competed to outdo each other with lurid crime stories and graphic accounts of murder were standard in Victorian tabloids.

There are numerous theories attempting to explain this phenomenon. One idea is that people like to see justice served. Numerous authors have made studies of past murders with the advantage of a historical perspective and modern science to find the truth, but there is a special fascination for cases that have not been conclusively resolved. Lizzie Borden—acquitted of the murder of her father and stepmother—and Jack the Ripper—whose identity has never been established—have both inspired many books that attempt to reconstruct and solve the crimes. In addition to historical accounts, there are also novels in which Lizzie Borden battles Lovecraftian monsters and Jack the Ripper goes up against a werewolf, Sherlock Holmes, and the Elephant Man. True crime stories can satisfy the interests of readers who want to see justice satisfied as well as those who want to solve the crimes themselves.

One of history’s deadliest serial killers was a married family man who managed to squeeze in 218 credited murders (and as many as 250) while working as a popular British physician. Harold Shipman began his murderous spree in 1972, and it’s believed he killed at least 71 patients while working at his first practice, and doubled that number at a second practice he joined after butting heads with colleagues who found him arrogant, brusque and overconfident.

HOW TO PROTECT YOURSELF

Self-defense actually starts before needing to react to the worst-case scenario—with self-awareness and preventive strategies. The first step is paying attention to your surroundings. That means only walking and parking in well-lit areas. Keep your keys (with a safety whistle, defense spray, or safety necklace, if you desire) easily accessible as you approach your car or front door.

While it's easy to think about obvious situations where you might be attacked, there are those that aren't so obvious, namely when you're in your car. Women have a tendency to get into their car and sit while they check messages—a habit that can actually spell trouble. "If a predator is watching you, this is the perfect opportunity for that person to get in the passenger side, [threaten you], and tell you where to go," says Jory. That's why as soon as you get in the car, as a matter of habit, you should immediately lock the doors and leave.

If you're at a party, stick with friends, and if you've left a drink out of sight even for a few seconds, get a new one. "Spiking a drink with a date rape drug can happen quickly," Jory says. When going on a date, tell family or friends where you're going, especially if this is a first date or blind date. If somebody pushes you to do something you don't want to do, know that you have a right to leave. And of course, charge your cell phone and keep it and a charger on you at all times.
